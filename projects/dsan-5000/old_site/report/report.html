Final report

This report is for a Non-technical audience, e.g. general public, executives, marketing teams, and clients. It should prioritize high-level insights, actionable results, and visualizations that convey the impact of data without requiring an understanding of complex methods. For instance, a CEO may want to know how a model affects business strategy or revenue, without diving into the underlying technical details.

Guidelines for Creating a Good Narrative Construction:

  1. Start with a Clear Purpose: Define the core message or objective of your narrative early on.
  2. Know Your Audience: Tailor your language, tone, and detail level to the interests and understanding of your target audience.
  3. Create a Strong Opening: Engage readers with an intriguing introduction or hook that sets the context and stakes.
  4. Maintain a Logical Flow: Organize your story with a clear beginning, middle, and end. Ensure smooth transitions between sections.
  5. Focus on Key Insights: Highlight the most important points or findings, avoiding unnecessary details or technical jargon.
  6. Use Data to Support the Story: Integrate data or evidence naturally to enhance credibility, but don’t overwhelm the narrative with too many numbers.
  7. Incorporate Visuals: Use charts, graphs, or images to simplify complex concepts and engage your audience.
  8. Conclude with Actionable Takeaways: End with a clear conclusion, summarizing key points and offering recommendations or next steps.
  9. Be Authentic and Relatable: Use storytelling techniques like anecdotes or examples to make the narrative more personal and engaging.
  10. Revise and Refine: Edit for clarity, coherence, and impact. Remove unnecessary content and ensure every part serves the overall purpose.

What to include in the report?

What you include sort of depends on the nature of your project, here are two possible cases.

Case-1: Academic oriented projects

When presenting an academic project to the general public, the focus is on communicating key findings and relevant insights in a way that is engaging and easy to understand. The goal is to break down complex research concepts while highlighting their real-world implications and societal benefits. Avoid technical jargon and focus on storytelling, impact, and accessibility.

1. Introduction

  • Purpose: Provide an accessible introduction to the project.
  • Background: Offer context by explaining the motivation behind the research and why it matters.
  • Key Questions: What questions are you trying to answer, and why are they important?

Example: “This study aims to understand how climate change affects local ecosystems, which is essential for protecting wildlife and biodiversity.”

2. Objective

  • Purpose: Clearly define the goal or purpose of the research.
  • Real-World Relevance: Explain how the project relates to issues or challenges the general public can understand (e.g., health, environment, technology).

Example: “Our objective is to identify the effects of air pollution on public health in urban areas to recommend better policies.”

3. Key Findings

  • Purpose: Present the most important insights from the research.
  • Plain Language: Avoid technical terms and explain the findings in a way that is easy to grasp.
  • Impact: Highlight how these findings affect real-world issues or help advance societal understanding.

Example: “We found that air pollution in cities contributes to a 20% higher risk of respiratory diseases, affecting children and the elderly the most.”

4. Methodology Overview (Simplified)

  • Purpose: Briefly explain how the research was conducted, but keep it high-level.
  • Plain Language: Describe the methods in an intuitive way, focusing on what the audience needs to know (e.g., data sources, experiments, or surveys).

Example: “We analyzed air quality data from 50 cities and conducted surveys with 10,000 residents to measure the health impact of pollution.”

5. Visualizations

  • Purpose: Use easy-to-understand graphs, infographics, or charts to help convey key findings.
  • Accessibility: Ensure all visuals are clearly labeled, with simple explanations of what the audience is seeing.
  • Engagement: Visuals should engage the public by making the findings more relatable and easier to grasp.

Example: A map showing regions most affected by pollution, paired with a bar chart depicting the increase in health risks.

6. Societal Implications

  • Purpose: Explain why the findings matter in the context of broader societal issues.
  • Relevance to Public: Connect the research to real-world outcomes that the general public cares about (e.g., public health, environmental protection, technological advances).
  • Potential Solutions: Offer suggestions or recommendations based on the research for improving outcomes.

Example: “This research shows the urgent need for cleaner air policies to reduce health risks, particularly in densely populated areas.”

7. Call to Action or Recommendations

  • Purpose: Provide actionable steps or policy recommendations based on the research.
  • Audience Engagement: Encourage readers to take action, support the cause, or further explore the topic.
  • Next Steps: Outline how the findings could be applied in the future or how further research could build on this work.

Example: “We recommend that city planners invest in green spaces and pollution-reducing technologies to protect public health.”

8. Conclusion

  • Purpose: Recap the main findings and their importance.
  • Broader Impact: Reinforce how this project contributes to a better understanding of the issue at hand and how it might shape future actions or policies.

Example: “By understanding the impacts of pollution on health, we can work together to create healthier, more sustainable cities.”

Final Tips:

  • Keep the language simple and engaging: Avoid complex terminology or deep dives into methodology unless it’s essential for understanding.
  • Use storytelling: Frame your findings as a narrative that highlights real-world impact and relevance.
  • Focus on visual aids: Non-technical audiences benefit from clear, visual representations of data and findings.
  • Relate to everyday life: Whenever possible, link the research to familiar experiences or commonly understood societal challenges.

Case-2: Buiness oriented projects

Here’s an outline of the sections to include in the report:

Overview: Creating a Data Science Report for Non-Technical Audiences

When preparing a data science report for non-technical audiences like executives, marketing teams, or clients, the focus should be on clear, actionable insights. Provide a high-level overview of findings, emphasizing their business impact and strategic relevance rather than technical details.

1. Executive Summary

  • Purpose: Briefly summarize the key findings and relevance to business goals.
  • Key Points: Highlight the top takeaways in 2-3 sentences.
  • Call to Action: Outline next steps or recommendations.

Example: “This report predicts customer churn rates and offers retention strategies to reduce churn by 15%.”

2. Objective

  • Purpose: State the problem or question the analysis addresses.
  • Business Context: Explain why this issue matters and its potential business impact.

Example: “This project aims to identify drivers of customer churn. Reducing churn by 5% could boost revenue by $2M.”

3. Key Insights

  • Purpose: Present the most impactful findings simply.
  • Actionable Insights: Focus on insights that influence decision-making.
  • Business Impact: Explain how insights affect business goals (e.g., cost savings, increased revenue).

Example: “Customers who interact with support at least twice within 30 days are 25% less likely to churn.”

4. Visualizations

  • Purpose: Use clear, intuitive graphs to convey key insights quickly.
  • Types of Visuals: Bar charts, line graphs, or heat maps to show trends or comparisons.
  • Context: Ensure visualizations have concise labels and explanations.

Example: A bar chart showing churn likelihood based on customer engagement.

5. Business Implications

  • Purpose: Explain how findings improve business outcomes.
  • Actionable Steps: Provide specific, actionable recommendations.
  • Strategic Relevance: Align insights with business objectives like revenue growth or cost reduction.

Example: “Focus retention efforts on low-engagement customers through personalized discounts or support.”

6. Recommendations

  • Purpose: Provide clear, actionable recommendations.
  • Next Steps: Outline immediate actions and timelines.
  • Business Projections: Quantify the impact of recommendations (e.g., increased retention, revenue growth).

Example: “Implement an automated retention campaign, expected to reduce churn by 10%, increasing revenue by $500K annually.”

7. Conclusion

  • Purpose: Summarize key findings and their business impact.
  • Call to Action: Provide a clear recommendation for next steps.

Example: “Focus on key drivers of churn to reduce customer loss and boost profitability.”

8. Appendix (Optional)

  • Purpose: Include additional charts or detailed explanations for those seeking deeper insights.
  • Simplified Methods: Provide non-technical descriptions of models or methods, focusing on their relevance to findings.

Final Tips

  • Simplicity: Avoid jargon; focus on business-relevant insights.
  • Be Visual: Use charts or graphs over dense text.
  • Emphasize Impact: Always link data insights to business outcomes.
